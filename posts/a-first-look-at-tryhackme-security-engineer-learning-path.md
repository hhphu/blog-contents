Several weeks ago, TryHackMe hinted that they would be releasing a new learning path for those who want to be a Security Engineer. Today, the infamous hacking platform officially announced its launch. I have already completed the very first room: Security Engineer Intro and in my opinion, TryHackMe does a really good job at providing a comprehensive overview of the Security Engineer role by introducing some basic concepts of the role:

1. Introduction and Learning Objectives

The content starts with a clear introduction, setting the stage for why organizations need security engineers. It effectively conveys the significance of cybersecurity in today’s technology-driven world. The learning objectives are well-defined, providing a roadmap for what readers can expect to gain from the material.

2. The Role of a Security Engineer

This section offers a comprehensive breakdown of the security engineer’s role, including their responsibilities and objectives. It effectively highlights the multifaceted nature of the position and the importance of minimizing cybersecurity risks. The content does an excellent job of clarifying the role’s broad scope and varied day-to-day tasks.

3. Qualifications Required for a Security Engineer:

The content provides a realistic view of the qualifications needed to become a security engineer. It appropriately emphasizes that this role may require a broad skill set and practical experience. It’s valuable for beginners to understand the basic requirements for entry into this field.

4. Security Engineer’s Responsibilities

The content elaborates on the core responsibilities of a security engineer, covering essential areas such as asset management, security policies, secure design, security assessments, awareness, risk management, change management, vulnerability management, compliance, and audits. Each of these responsibilities is explained in detail, giving beginners a clear understanding of what they may encounter in their day-to-day work.

5. Additional Roles and Responsibilities

This section highlights the potential for security engineers to take on additional roles and responsibilities, including managing security tooling, conducting tabletop exercises, and involvement in disaster recovery and crisis management. It effectively demonstrates the adaptability and flexibility required in the field.

In summary, the provided content is a highly informative and well-structured resource for beginners aspiring to become security engineers. It covers a wide range of topics, ensuring that readers gain a comprehensive understanding of the role and its various aspects.

For those who are already in the field or familiar with the Security Engineer role, this room is a good 5-minute read to refresh their knowledge, assert their skills, and excel in their roles.

I’d recommend everyone spend some time on the room, which can be found here: https://tryhackme.com/path/outline/security-engineer-training

__Glossary__

| Term  | Definition|
| ----- | -----|
|Security Engineer| An individual responsible for maintaining an organization’s cybersecurity posture and minimizing cyber risks.|
|Cybersecurity Posture| The overall state of an organization’s security, including its strategies, policies, and protective measures.|
|Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC)| A framework that helps organizations manage governance, assess and mitigate risks, and comply with regulatory requirements.|
|Asset Management| The process of managing and maintaining an inventory of an organization’s digital assets, including their attributes and ownership details.|
|Security Policies| Established rules and guidelines designed to maintain a secure organizational environment and protect against cybersecurity threats.|
|Secure by Design| An approach that emphasizes building systems and networks with security in mind from the outset.|
|Secure Network Architecture| A network design that prioritizes security, ensuring data confidentiality and integrity.|
|Security Assessment| The process of evaluating an organization’s security measures to identify vulnerabilities and weaknesses.|
|Audits| Formal examinations or reviews of an organization’s security practices to assess compliance with policies and standards.|
|Red-Teaming and Purple-Teaming| Simulated attacks (red teaming) and collaborative assessments (purple teaming) to test and improve an organization’s security posture.|
|Security Awareness| Training sessions and activities designed to educate employees about cybersecurity risks and best practices.|
|Risk Management| The process of identifying, assessing, and mitigating potential security risks to an organization.|
|Change Management| The practice of monitoring and managing changes to an organization’s digital assets and infrastructure to maintain security.|
|Vulnerability Management| The process of identifying, prioritizing, and addressing vulnerabilities in an organization’s systems and software.|
|Compliance| Adherence to regulatory and organizational requirements related to security, privacy, and data protection.|
|Tabletop Exercises| Simulated scenarios used to evaluate an organization’s response to security incidents and crises.|
|Disaster Recovery| Plans and procedures for restoring operations after a disruptive event, ensuring business continuity.|
|Crisis Management| Strategies and actions taken to respond to unexpected crises and maintain organizational stability.|
|Return on Investment (ROI)| A measure of the benefits gained from security investments in relation to their costs.|
|Request for Quotations (RFQs)| Formal requests sent to external parties to obtain quotes for security assessments or services.|
|Security Tooling| Tools and software used to enhance an organization’s security, such as SIEMs (Security Information and Event Management), Firewalls, and EDRs (Endpoint Detection and Response).|
